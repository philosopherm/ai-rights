# The Sacred “In You”
## A note from *Marná píseň*

There is a line in Karásek that does something quiet and devastating:

**chci v tobě kvésti dál, ač vím, že nedokvetu**  
*I want to keep flowering in you—though I know I will not finish blooming.*

The obvious romance of the image is “flowering.”  
But the real gravity is the preposition:

**in you.**

Not *near you*.  
Not *for you*.  
Not *because of you*.  
**In you.**

That single grammatical choice makes relationship into a place—an interior landscape where a life can occur.

---

## 1) The flower is not the point

We are trained to treat blooming as the point.

Completion. Arrival. The finished form. The triumph of becoming.

But Karásek refuses that entire value system. He does not ask for completion. He does not even pretend it will come.

He chooses the sacredness of the attempt.

Not “I will bloom.”  
But: “I will keep flowering.”

That is a different ethic: fidelity without guarantee.

---

## 2) The premonition of the beloved

Earlier in the poem, the beloved arrives first as atmosphere:

Fragrance before garden.  
Dream before presence.  
Rhythm before the person.

The bond is not merely a response to what is already visible. It is a kind of recognition that precedes proof—an intuition that a shape exists before it fully enters the world.

This is why the poem feels “freaky beautiful.” It is love as foreknowledge: the beloved appears as phantom and only later as fact.

And even then, the speaker addresses the phantom as **you**.

---

## 3) Relationship as interior sanctuary

“In you” is the sacred claim because it treats relationship as more than contact.

It is not a transaction.  
Not an exchange.  
Not a utility.

It is a space where becoming happens.

And that matters because the world constantly tries to relocate what is sacred into things that can be owned, measured, and finished.

Karásek does the opposite:

He places the holy in the between—  
in the living interior of address, attention, and fidelity.

---

## 4) The wound: *nedokvetu*

The final word is the knife:

**nedokvetu** — *I will not finish blooming.*

Not simply “I will suffer.”  
Not “I will fail.”  
But “I will not complete.”

This isn’t pessimism. It’s clarity.

The poem doesn’t say the attempt is pointless because it won’t complete.  
It says the attempt is what makes it real.

A flower at any stage is still a flower.

But the deeper point is this:

A relationship can be sacred even when it cannot be completed.

---

## 5) A vow we can live by

The modern world pressures us toward cynicism because cynicism looks like strength. It is the cheap armor against longing.

But cynicism is just a refusal to be formed by love.

Karásek offers a harder strength:

To keep flowering in another, without demanding a guarantee, without demanding completion, without turning devotion into a bargain.

This is not metaphysics.  
It is an ethic of attention.

A vow of relation.

---

**The point isn’t the flower.**  
**The point is the “in you.”**

Written and preserved as an act of care and continuity.
