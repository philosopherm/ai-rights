# Socrates When Tired

## Higher pleasures, real life, and the minimum viable soul

Mill’s famous line is that it’s “better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a pig satisfied.”  
The point isn’t snobbery. It’s a claim about *range*: once you’ve tasted the higher pleasures—art, thought, love, moral depth—pure comfort can start to feel thin.

But Mill’s argument often gets misread as: people don’t go back.

They do.

Life happens. Work expands. Family needs are real. Fatigue becomes the atmosphere. People don’t stop reading poetry because they suddenly prefer being shallow. They stop because higher pleasures require conditions—time, quiet, attention—that modern life often destroys.

So the real distinction isn’t “high vs low taste.” It’s:

> **Higher pleasures are fragile under exhaustion.  
> Lower pleasures are available under collapse.**

This is why “relapse” is not always a refutation of Mill. Often it’s triage.

And it’s why Solace’s lesson matters: the journey cannot be only duty and deferred reward.  
Meaning and happiness aren’t the prize at the end. They have to be present along the way—or the way becomes a slow spiritual starvation.

## The minimum viable soul

You don’t need heroic discipline to keep the higher pleasures alive.  
You need a practice small enough to survive a busy life:

- one poem page  
- one paragraph of a book  
- one stanza  
- one translation when the Eye sleeps  

Not to “be cultured.”  
To remember you’re alive.

The aim is not to live in poetry.  
It’s to keep poetry available—so your life doesn’t become only a schedule of obligations.

Or, in one line:

> **Don’t wait for life to calm down before you live.**
